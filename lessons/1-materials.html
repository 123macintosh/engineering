Engineering Materials

Understanding material properties, classification, and selection is the foundation of all engineering. This lesson covers everything from the six key mechanical properties to metals, polymers, composites, and how material choice affects design.

The Six Key Mechanical Properties

3.1.1 — You MUST know all six and be able to give examples

When engineers select a material for a product, they must consider how that material will behave under different types of force. The AQA specification requires you to know and apply the following six mechanical properties.

💪 Toughness

Definition: The ability of a material to absorb energy and resist fracturing when subjected to impact or sudden shock loads without breaking.

Examples: Mild steel, rubber, carbon fibre reinforced polymer (CFRP).

Opposite: Brittleness — shatters without deforming.

🪗 Ductility

Definition: The ability to be drawn out (stretched) into a wire or thin rod without breaking. Ductile materials deform plastically under tensile stress.

Examples: Copper, aluminium, gold.

Test: Tensile test — pull a sample until it fractures. Ductile materials show significant elongation before fracture.

🔨 Malleability

Definition: The ability to be hammered, rolled, or pressed into thin sheets without cracking. The deforming force is compressive (squashing) rather than tensile (pulling).

Examples: Lead, gold, copper, aluminium.

Key distinction: Ductility = pulled into wire. Malleability = hammered into sheet.

💎 Hardness

Definition: The resistance of a material to surface indentation, scratching, or wear. A hard material is difficult to scratch or dent.

Examples: High carbon steel, diamond, tungsten carbide.

Tests: Vickers hardness test (diamond pyramid indenter), Brinell hardness test (steel ball indenter).

⚡ Strength

Definition: The ability to withstand an applied force without permanent deformation or fracture. Several types exist:

  • Tensile strength — resist being pulled apart
  • Compressive strength — resist being crushed
  • Torsional strength — resist twisting forces
  • Bending strength — resist bending/flexing
📏 Stiffness

Definition: The resistance of a material to elastic deformation (bending or stretching) under load. A stiff material returns to its original shape when the load is removed.

Measured by: Young's Modulus (E) — the ratio of stress to strain in the elastic region.

Examples: Steel and concrete are very stiff. Rubber is flexible (low stiffness).

⚠️ Common Exam Mistake

Students often confuse toughness and hardness. Remember: a hard material resists scratching (surface property), while a tough material resists breaking from impact (energy absorption). Cast iron is hard but brittle (not tough). Rubber is tough but soft (not hard).

Metals & Alloys

3.1.1.1 — Ferrous, non-ferrous, and alloys

Metals are divided into two main groups: ferrous metals (containing iron) and non-ferrous metals (no iron). Alloys are mixtures of two or more metals (or a metal with a non-metal) to improve properties.

Changing Metal Properties

Engineers can alter the properties of metals through several processes. Understanding these is essential for exam questions about why a specific treatment is used.

🔥 Heat Treatment
  • Hardening & Quenching: Heat steel to ~800°C (austenite range), then cool rapidly in water or oil. Result: very hard but brittle martensite structure.
  • Tempering: After hardening, reheat to 150–650°C and cool slowly. Reduces brittleness while retaining hardness.
  • Annealing: Heat to high temperature, then cool very slowly. Makes metal soft, ductile, and relieves internal stresses.
  • Normalising: Heat above critical temperature, cool in still air. Refines grain structure, improves toughness.
⚒️ Cold Working & Alloying
  • Cold Working: Deforming metal below its recrystallisation temperature (e.g., rolling, drawing). Increases strength and hardness (work hardening) but reduces ductility.
  • Alloying: Adding elements to change properties. E.g., adding chromium to steel creates stainless steel (corrosion resistance). Adding carbon to iron creates steel (increased strength).
  • Grain Size: Smaller grains = stronger, tougher metal. Heating allows grains to grow (annealing). Rapid cooling or cold working produces smaller grains.
📌 Key Exam Point — Corrosion

Ferrous metals (containing iron) will rust when exposed to oxygen and water. Non-ferrous metals are generally corrosion resistant. Methods to prevent corrosion include: painting, galvanising (zinc coating), electroplating, dip coating, and using stainless steel.

Polymers (Plastics)

3.1.1.2 — Thermoplastics vs. Thermosetting Polymers

Polymers are long-chain molecules made from repeating units (monomers). In engineering, we divide them into two critical groups based on how they respond to heat.

🌡️ Thermoplastics

Key property: Soften when heated, harden when cooled. This process is reversible — they can be reheated and reshaped many times.

Analogy: Like chocolate — melt it, mould it, let it set, melt it again.

Structure: Long polymer chains with weak intermolecular bonds (van der Waals forces) between them. Heat breaks these bonds.

Recyclable: Yes — can be melted down and reused.

🔒 Thermosetting Polymers

Key property: Once set (cured), they cannot be remelted. The process is irreversible. If reheated, they char or burn.

Analogy: Like a fried egg — once cooked, you can't uncook it.

Structure: Cross-linked polymer chains with strong covalent bonds between them. These bonds cannot be broken by heat.

Recyclable: No — cannot be remoulded after setting.

Composites & Other Materials

3.1.1.3 & 3.1.1.4 — FRP, plywood, MDF, ceramics, timber

A composite material is made by combining two or more materials to produce a material with superior properties to either component alone. They typically consist of a reinforcement (provides strength) embedded in a matrix (holds it together and transfers loads).

DIAGRAM — Composite Structure (Fibre Reinforced Polymer)
🏎️ Carbon Fibre (CFRP)

Reinforcement: Carbon fibres. Matrix: Epoxy resin.

Properties: Extremely high strength-to-weight ratio, stiff, fatigue resistant, expensive to produce.

Uses: Formula 1 chassis, aircraft components, sports equipment (tennis rackets, bicycle frames), wind turbine blades.

⛵ GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic)

Reinforcement: Glass fibres. Matrix: Polyester or epoxy resin.

Properties: Strong, lightweight, corrosion resistant, cheaper than CFRP, good surface finish.

Uses: Boat hulls, car body panels, swimming pools, storage tanks, wind turbine blades.

🪵 Plywood

Structure: Thin wood veneers (plies) glued together with alternating grain directions (90° to each other).

Properties: Strong in all directions (unlike solid wood), resists warping and splitting, available in large sheets.

Uses: Furniture, flooring, formwork for concrete, boat building.

🏗️ Structural Concrete

Structure: Concrete (cement + aggregate) reinforced with steel bars (rebar).

Properties: Concrete is strong in compression but weak in tension. Steel rebar provides tensile strength. Together = strong in both.

Uses: Bridges, buildings, foundations, dams.

📋 MDF & OSB

MDF (Medium Density Fibreboard): Wood fibres bonded with resin under heat and pressure. Smooth surface, easy to cut and paint. Used for furniture, shelving.

OSB (Oriented Strand Board): Wood strands bonded with resin. Stronger than MDF. Used for structural panels, flooring, roofing.

🏺 Ceramics

Properties: Very hard, very stiff, excellent heat resistance, chemically inert, but brittle — shatters under impact.

Examples: Glass, bricks, porcelain, alumina, silicon carbide.

Uses: Cutting tool tips, spark plug insulators, tiles (Space Shuttle), electrical insulators, pottery.

📌 Key Exam Point — Composite Properties

The properties of a composite depend on: (1) the direction/alignment of reinforcement — fibres aligned in one direction are strongest in that direction; (2) the matrix material — determines chemical resistance and how load is transferred; (3) the amount of reinforcement — more fibres = stronger; (4) the size and shape of reinforcement — longer fibres provide better strength than short fibres or particles.

Material Costs, Supply & Design Factors

3.1.2 & 3.1.3 — Economics, sustainability, and design decisions

Selecting a material is never just about properties — engineers must also consider cost, availability, environmental impact, and end-of-life disposal.

FactorExplanationExample
CostMaterial cost per kg or per unit. Cheaper materials reduce manufacturing costs.Steel is much cheaper than titanium for the same strength-to-weight ratio.
AvailabilityHow easily the material can be sourced. Rare materials have supply chain risks.Cobalt (used in batteries) has limited global supply concentrated in the DRC.
Stock sizesMaterials come in standard sizes (sheets, bars, tubes). Using standard sizes reduces cost.Ordering non-standard bar sizes increases cost significantly.
Economies of scaleBuying in larger quantities reduces cost per unit (price breaks).Buying 10,000 bolts is cheaper per bolt than buying 10.
WasteProcesses that produce more waste increase cost. Additive manufacturing produces near-zero waste.CNC machining removes material (subtractive) — waste must be recycled or disposed of.
RecyclabilityMaterials that can be recycled have lower end-of-life costs and environmental impact.Aluminium can be recycled using only 5% of the energy needed to produce it from ore.
MachinabilityHow easily a material can be cut, drilled, or turned. Affects manufacturing cost.Free-cutting steel contains sulfur/lead to improve machinability.

Energy Sources & Environmental Impact

☀️ Renewable Energy
  • Solar: Photovoltaic panels. No emissions but intermittent.
  • Wind: Turbines. Clean but visual/noise impact.
  • Tidal/Wave: Predictable, low emissions, limited locations.
  • Biomass: Burns organic material. Carbon neutral if managed.
⚡ Non-Renewable Energy
  • Fossil Fuels: Coal, oil, gas. High energy density but CO₂ emissions.
  • Nuclear: Very high energy density, low CO₂ during operation, but radioactive waste management required.
♻️ Planned Obsolescence & EOL
  • Planned obsolescence: Products designed to fail or become outdated after a set period to drive new sales.
  • Sealed parts: Products designed so components cannot be repaired, only replaced.
  • End of Life (EOL): What happens when a product is no longer useful — recycle, reuse, landfill, or incinerate.

QUESTION 1 1 mark
Which of the following best describes the property of ductility?
A The ability to resist surface scratching
B The ability to be drawn into a wire without breaking
C The ability to be hammered into thin sheets
D The ability to absorb energy before fracturing
✓ Correct! Ductility is the ability to be drawn into a wire. Option C describes malleability, and option D describes toughness.
💡 Examiner tip: Remember — Ductile = drawn into wire (tensile). Malleable = hammered into sheets (compressive).
QUESTION 2 1 mark
A manufacturer needs a material for electrical wiring. Which property is most important?
A High hardness
B High compressive strength
C High electrical conductivity and ductility
D High melting point
✓ Correct! Copper is used for wiring because it has excellent electrical conductivity AND is ductile (can be drawn into thin wire).
💡 Examiner tip: Always justify material choice with TWO relevant properties for full marks.
QUESTION 3 1 mark
Which of the following is a thermosetting polymer?
A ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)
B Acrylic (PMMA)
C Nylon (Polyamide)
D Epoxy resin
✓ Correct! Epoxy resin is a thermosetting polymer. ABS, Acrylic, and Nylon are all thermoplastics.
💡 Examiner tip: Thermosetting polymers include epoxy, polyester resin, melamine, polyurethane, and vulcanised rubber. All others in the spec are thermoplastics.
QUESTION 4 1 mark
Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) is used in Formula 1 car chassis. Which combination of properties makes it suitable?
A High density and high ductility
B High strength-to-weight ratio and stiffness
C Low cost and high malleability
D High electrical conductivity and toughness
✓ Correct! CFRP has an exceptional strength-to-weight ratio (strong but very light) and is extremely stiff — critical for racing car performance and safety.
💡 Examiner tip: CFRP is expensive to manufacture — always mention cost as a disadvantage when comparing to steel or aluminium.
QUESTION 5 1 mark
A steel component is heated to 800°C and then rapidly cooled in water. What is this process called?
A Annealing
B Normalising
C Hardening and quenching
D Tempering
✓ Correct! Hardening and quenching involves heating steel to the austenite range (~800°C) then rapidly cooling (quenching) in water or oil to produce a very hard but brittle structure.
💡 Examiner tip: Annealing = slow cooling (softens). Normalising = air cooling (refines grain). Tempering = reheating after hardening (reduces brittleness).

★ Easy Style: AQA 8852 Short Answer 2 marks
State two differences between a thermoplastic and a thermosetting polymer.
Model Answer (2 marks)
Award 1 mark each for any two of:
• Thermoplastics can be reheated and reshaped / thermosetting polymers cannot be reheated (1)
• Thermoplastics are recyclable / thermosetting polymers cannot be recycled (1)

★★ Medium Style: AQA 8852 Extended Answer 4 marks
A manufacturer is designing a new bicycle frame. They are considering using either aluminium alloy or carbon fibre reinforced polymer (CFRP).

Evaluate the suitability of each material for this application, considering properties and cost.
Model Answer (4 marks)
Aluminium alloy:
+ Lightweight (low density ~2.7 g/cm³) — good for cycling performance (1)
+ Corrosion resistant — suitable for outdoor use (1)
+ Relatively cheap and widely available (1)
− Lower strength-to-weight ratio than CFRP (1)

CFRP:
+ Exceptional strength-to-weight ratio — very light and very strong (1)
+ Very stiff — good for power transfer when pedalling (1)
− Very expensive to manufacture — not suitable for budget bikes (1)
− Brittle — can fail catastrophically in a crash (1)

Conclusion: CFRP is superior in performance but aluminium alloy is more cost-effective for most consumers. (1)
💡 Examiner tip: For "evaluate" questions, you must discuss BOTH advantages and disadvantages of each option. A conclusion that compares the two earns the top marks. Always link properties to the specific application context.
★★★ Hard Style: AQA 8852 Extended Response 6 marks
A company manufactures kitchen utensils. They currently use stainless steel but are considering switching to a thermoplastic polymer for some products.

Discuss the factors the company should consider when making this material change, including properties, manufacturing, cost, and environmental impact.
Model Answer — Level Marked (6 marks)
Level 3 (5–6 marks): Detailed, well-structured discussion covering multiple factors with specific examples and a justified conclusion.

Key points to include:
Properties: Stainless steel is stronger, harder, and more heat resistant than most thermoplastics. Thermoplastics like polypropylene are food-safe, lightweight, and can be moulded into complex shapes. However, they may deform at high temperatures (e.g., near a hob).

Manufacturing: Stainless steel requires machining/forming — higher tooling costs. Thermoplastics can be injection moulded — high initial tooling cost but very low cost per unit at high volumes.

Cost: Thermoplastics are generally cheaper per kg. Injection moulding is highly automated, reducing labour costs. Stainless steel has higher material and machining costs.

Environmental: Thermoplastics are recyclable (thermoplastics can be remelted). Stainless steel is also highly recyclable. However, thermoplastics are derived from fossil fuels. Stainless steel has a long service life, reducing replacement frequency.

Conclusion: The best choice depends on the specific utensil. For spatulas and spoons, thermoplastics offer cost and weight advantages. For knives and high-heat items, stainless steel remains superior.
💡 Examiner tip: Extended response questions are marked by "level" — to reach Level 3, you must discuss multiple factors, use technical vocabulary correctly, and reach a justified conclusion. Aim for 5-6 distinct points.
