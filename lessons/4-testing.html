Testing & Investigation

Engineers must test materials and products to ensure they meet specifications. This lesson covers destructive and non-destructive testing methods, quality control, aerodynamics, and how to plan and carry out investigations.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between destructive and non-destructive testing (NDT)
  • Describe hardness, tensile, impact, and fatigue testing methods
  • Explain NDT methods: visual, dye penetrant, magnetic particle, ultrasonic, X-ray
  • Understand quality control, tolerances, and inspection methods
  • Describe aerodynamic principles and how they affect engineering design
  • Plan and evaluate engineering investigations

Destructive Testing

3.4.1 — Tests that damage or destroy the sample

Destructive testing involves applying forces or conditions to a sample until it fails. The sample is destroyed in the process. These tests provide precise data about material properties but cannot be used on finished products.

💪 Tensile Testing

Purpose: Measures tensile strength, yield strength, Young's modulus, ductility, and elongation at fracture.

Method: A standardised test piece (dog-bone shape) is gripped in a tensile testing machine. Load is applied at a controlled rate until fracture. A stress-strain graph is plotted.

Key measurements:

  • Yield point: Stress at which permanent deformation begins
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS): Maximum stress before fracture
  • % Elongation: Measure of ductility
💎 Hardness Testing

Vickers Hardness Test: A diamond pyramid indenter is pressed into the surface under a known load. The diagonal of the indentation is measured. HV = load ÷ area of indentation.

Brinell Hardness Test: A hardened steel ball is pressed into the surface. BHN = load ÷ area of indentation.

Rockwell Hardness Test: Measures depth of penetration. Faster than Vickers/Brinell.

Note: Hardness tests are considered semi-destructive — they leave a small indentation but don't destroy the part.

⚡ Charpy Impact Test

Purpose: Measures the toughness (energy absorbed before fracture) of a material, especially at different temperatures.

Method: A notched test piece is placed horizontally in a vice. A heavy pendulum swings and strikes the specimen. The energy absorbed = difference between initial and final height of pendulum.

Result: Energy in Joules. Higher value = tougher material.

Application: Critical for materials used in cold environments (e.g., ship hulls, pipelines in Arctic conditions).

🔄 Fatigue Testing

Purpose: Determines how many cycles of repeated loading a material can withstand before failure. Most engineering failures are due to fatigue, not static overload.

Method: A rotating beam test applies alternating bending stress. The number of cycles to failure is recorded at different stress levels.

S-N Curve: Plots stress amplitude vs. number of cycles to failure. The endurance limit is the stress below which the material will not fail regardless of cycles.

Examples: Aircraft wings, bridges, rotating shafts, springs.

Non-Destructive Testing (NDT)

3.4.2 — Testing without damaging the component

Non-destructive testing allows engineers to inspect components for defects without destroying them. This is essential for safety-critical components (aircraft, bridges, pressure vessels) that must be inspected while remaining in service.

Quality Control & Tolerances

3.4.3 — Ensuring parts meet specifications

Quality control (QC) ensures that manufactured parts meet the required specification. A key concept is tolerance — the acceptable range of variation in a dimension.

📐 Tolerances — Key Concepts

A shaft might be specified as Ø25 ±0.05 mm. This means:

  • Nominal size: 25 mm (the target dimension)
  • Upper limit: 25.05 mm (maximum acceptable)
  • Lower limit: 24.95 mm (minimum acceptable)
  • Tolerance: 0.10 mm (total range = upper – lower)

Parts outside the tolerance are rejected. Tighter tolerances cost more to achieve.

Statistical Process Control (SPC)

📊 Control Charts

In mass production, it is impossible to inspect every part. Instead, samples are taken at regular intervals and measured. The results are plotted on a control chart.

Control charts have Upper Control Limits (UCL) and Lower Control Limits (LCL). If sample measurements fall outside these limits, the process is stopped and investigated.

✅ Quality Assurance vs. Quality Control

Quality Control (QC): Inspection and testing of finished products to find defects. Reactive — finds problems after they occur.

Quality Assurance (QA): Systematic processes to prevent defects from occurring in the first place. Proactive — prevents problems.

ISO 9001: International quality management standard. Companies certified to ISO 9001 have demonstrated systematic quality management.

Aerodynamics & Fluid Mechanics

3.4.4 — How shape affects forces in fluids

Aerodynamics is the study of how air (and other fluids) flows around objects. Understanding aerodynamics is crucial for designing cars, aircraft, bridges, and buildings.

DIAGRAM — Aerodynamic Forces on an Aircraft
💨 Drag

Drag is the aerodynamic force that opposes motion through a fluid. It acts parallel to the direction of motion (backwards).

Types:

  • Form drag: Due to the shape of the object. Streamlined shapes have low form drag.
  • Skin friction drag: Due to friction between fluid and surface. Smooth surfaces reduce this.
  • Induced drag: Created as a by-product of generating lift.

Drag ∝ v² — doubling speed quadruples drag force.

🚀 Lift

Lift is the aerodynamic force perpendicular to the direction of motion. It is generated by the aerofoil (wing) shape.

Bernoulli's Principle: Air flowing over the curved upper surface of a wing travels faster than air below. Faster flow = lower pressure. The pressure difference creates lift.

Angle of attack: Increasing the angle of attack increases lift (up to the stall angle).

🏎️ Streamlining

A streamlined shape allows fluid to flow smoothly around it with minimal turbulence, reducing drag.

Drag coefficient (Cd): A dimensionless measure of aerodynamic efficiency. Lower Cd = less drag.

  • Flat plate (face-on): Cd ≈ 1.2
  • Sphere: Cd ≈ 0.47
  • Teardrop/aerofoil: Cd ≈ 0.04
  • Modern car: Cd ≈ 0.25–0.35
🌊 Bernoulli's Equation
Bernoulli's Principle
P + ½ρv² = constant
P=pressure · ρ=density · v=velocity

As fluid velocity increases, pressure decreases (and vice versa). This explains lift, carburettors, and venturi meters.

Wind Tunnels & Testing

🌬️ Wind Tunnel Testing

A wind tunnel is used to test the aerodynamic properties of models or full-size objects. Air is blown over a stationary model (equivalent to the model moving through still air).

What is measured: Drag force, lift force, pressure distribution, flow visualisation (using smoke or tufts).

Uses: Car design, aircraft design, bridge design, building design, sports equipment.

💻 CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics)

Modern engineering uses CFD software to simulate fluid flow around objects using mathematical models. This is faster and cheaper than physical wind tunnel testing.

Advantages: No physical model needed, can test many variations quickly, detailed flow visualisation.

Disadvantages: Results must be validated against physical tests; complex geometries require powerful computers.

QUESTION 1 1 mark
Which NDT method can ONLY be used on ferromagnetic materials?
A Dye penetrant testing
B Ultrasonic testing
C Magnetic particle inspection
D X-ray radiography
✓ Correct! Magnetic particle inspection requires the material to be magnetised, which is only possible with ferromagnetic materials (iron, steel, nickel).
💡 Examiner tip: MPI = ferromagnetic only. Dye penetrant = non-porous surface defects. UT = internal defects in any material. X-ray = internal defects, radiation hazard.
QUESTION 2 1 mark
A shaft is specified as Ø30 ±0.1 mm. What is the tolerance?
A 0.1 mm
B 0.2 mm
C 30 mm
D 30.1 mm
✓ Correct! Tolerance = Upper limit − Lower limit = 30.1 − 29.9 = 0.2 mm. The ±0.1 means 0.1 above AND 0.1 below the nominal size.
💡 Examiner tip: Tolerance = total range = (nominal + upper deviation) − (nominal − lower deviation) = 2 × deviation for symmetric tolerances.
QUESTION 3 1 mark
The Charpy impact test measures which material property?
A Hardness
B Tensile strength
C Toughness (impact energy)
D Fatigue life
✓ Correct! The Charpy test measures toughness — the energy absorbed before fracture. The result is given in Joules.
💡 Examiner tip: Charpy = toughness. Vickers/Brinell = hardness. Tensile test = tensile strength, ductility, Young's modulus. Rotating beam = fatigue.
QUESTION 4 1 mark
According to Bernoulli's principle, what happens to the pressure of a fluid as its velocity increases?
A Pressure increases
B Pressure decreases
C Pressure stays the same
D Pressure doubles
✓ Correct! Bernoulli's principle states that as fluid velocity increases, pressure decreases. This is the basis for how aircraft wings generate lift.
💡 Examiner tip: Higher speed = lower pressure. This is why air flowing faster over the top of a wing creates lower pressure above, and the pressure difference generates lift.
QUESTION 5 1 mark
Which inspection tool would be most appropriate for checking if a shaft diameter of 25mm is within a tolerance of ±0.02mm?
A Steel rule
B Vernier caliper
C Micrometer
D Dial test indicator
✓ Correct! A micrometer reads to 0.01mm (or 0.001mm for a digital micrometer), which is accurate enough for a ±0.02mm tolerance. A vernier caliper reads to 0.02mm — borderline, but a micrometer is more appropriate.
💡 Examiner tip: Match the measuring tool accuracy to the tolerance. Rule of thumb: measuring tool should be at least 10× more accurate than the tolerance.
