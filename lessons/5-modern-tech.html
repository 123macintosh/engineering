CAD — Computer Aided Design

3.5.1 — Digital design tools and their advantages

CAD software allows engineers to create precise 2D drawings and 3D models of components and assemblies on a computer. It has largely replaced manual drawing on paper in professional engineering.

💻 What CAD Can Do
  • 2D drawing: Orthographic projections, detail drawings with dimensions and tolerances
  • 3D modelling: Solid models, surface models, assemblies
  • Simulation: FEA (Finite Element Analysis) — stress analysis, thermal analysis
  • Rendering: Photorealistic images for presentations and marketing
  • Animation: Mechanism simulation, assembly sequences
  • Drawing generation: Automatic 2D drawings from 3D models
✅ Advantages of CAD over Manual Drawing
  • Designs can be easily edited without redrawing from scratch
  • Designs can be shared electronically worldwide instantly
  • Automatic checking for errors and interferences in assemblies
  • Precise dimensions — no measurement errors from drawing
  • Design history (parametric modelling) — changing one dimension updates the whole model
  • Can be directly linked to CAM for manufacturing (CAD/CAM workflow)
  • Multiple design variations can be tested without physical prototypes

CNC (Computer Numerical Control) Machines

CNC machines are controlled by a computer program (G-code) rather than a human operator. They can produce complex parts with high accuracy and repeatability.

✅ Advantages of CNC
  • High accuracy and repeatability — every part identical
  • Can run 24/7 without breaks (lights-out manufacturing)
  • Complex 3D shapes impossible to produce manually
  • Reduced labour costs for high-volume production
  • Quick changeover — just change the program
  • Consistent quality — no human error
❌ Disadvantages of CNC
  • High initial cost of machines
  • Skilled programmers required to write G-code / set up CAM
  • Not cost-effective for one-off or very small batches
  • Machine downtime for maintenance or tool changes
  • Job displacement — fewer manual machining jobs

Automation & Robotics

3.5.3 — Industrial robots and automated systems

Automation involves using machines and control systems to perform tasks with minimal human intervention. Industrial robots are programmable machines that can perform a variety of tasks in manufacturing.

🤖 Industrial Robots

Industrial robots typically have 6 degrees of freedom (axes of movement), allowing them to reach any position and orientation within their work envelope.

Common applications:

  • Spot welding (car body assembly)
  • Paint spraying (consistent, no overspray)
  • Pick and place (assembly, packaging)
  • Material handling (loading/unloading CNC machines)
  • Inspection (vision systems)
🏭 Flexible Manufacturing Systems (FMS)

An FMS is a group of CNC machines connected by automated material handling (conveyors, AGVs) and controlled by a central computer.

Advantages: Can produce a variety of parts without manual changeover; highly efficient; minimal work-in-progress; responds quickly to demand changes.

Example: A car manufacturer can switch between producing different car models on the same production line by changing the robot programs.

🔄 Just-In-Time (JIT) Manufacturing

JIT is a production strategy where materials and components arrive exactly when needed, minimising inventory and storage costs.

Advantages: Reduced storage costs, less waste, faster response to demand changes.

Disadvantages: Vulnerable to supply chain disruptions (e.g., COVID-19 showed the fragility of JIT).

🧠 Industry 4.0

The Fourth Industrial Revolution involves the integration of digital technologies into manufacturing:

  • IoT: Machines communicate with each other and with management systems
  • Big Data: Analysing production data to optimise processes
  • AI/Machine Learning: Predictive maintenance, quality control
  • Digital Twin: Virtual replica of a physical system for simulation and monitoring

Environmental Impact & Sustainability

3.5.4 — The circular economy and responsible engineering

Modern engineers must consider the environmental impact of their products throughout the entire product lifecycle — from raw material extraction to end-of-life disposal.

Smart Materials & New Technologies

3.5.5 — Materials and technologies that respond to their environment
🧠 Shape Memory Alloys (SMA)

SMAs (e.g., Nitinol — nickel-titanium) can be deformed at low temperature and then return to their original shape when heated above a critical temperature.

Uses: Medical stents, spectacle frames, actuators, orthodontic wires, pipe couplings.

🌡️ Thermochromic Materials

Change colour in response to temperature changes. The colour change is reversible.

Uses: Baby food spoons (indicate if food is too hot), mood rings, temperature-sensitive labels on packaging, forehead thermometers.

💡 Photochromic Materials

Change colour/opacity in response to light intensity (UV radiation). Reversible.

Uses: Photochromic lenses (transition glasses — darken in sunlight), UV-sensitive inks for security printing.

⚡ Piezoelectric Materials

Generate an electrical voltage when mechanically stressed (direct effect), or deform when a voltage is applied (converse effect).

Uses: Pressure sensors, ultrasonic transducers, energy harvesting from vibrations, inkjet printer heads, gas lighters.

🔋 Graphene

A single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice. Strongest material ever tested, excellent electrical conductor, transparent, and flexible.

Potential uses: Ultra-strong composites, flexible electronics, super-capacitors, water filtration membranes, next-generation batteries.

🏗️ Self-Healing Materials

Materials that can automatically repair damage (cracks, scratches) without human intervention.

Examples: Self-healing concrete (contains bacteria that produce limestone to fill cracks); self-healing polymers (contain microcapsules of healing agent).

Uses: Infrastructure, aerospace, automotive coatings.

QUESTION 1 1 mark
What does CAM software produce that is used by a CNC machine?
A A 3D CAD model
B G-code (machine instructions / toolpaths)
C A physical prototype
D A quality control report
✓ Correct! CAM software takes a CAD model and generates G-code — the set of machine instructions (toolpaths, speeds, feeds) that the CNC machine follows.
💡 Examiner tip: CAD = Design. CAM = Manufacturing instructions. CNC = Machine that follows those instructions.
QUESTION 2 1 mark
Which smart material changes colour in response to temperature?
A Photochromic material
B Thermochromic material
C Piezoelectric material
D Shape memory alloy
✓ Correct! Thermochromic materials change colour with temperature (thermo = heat). Photochromic = light. Piezoelectric = pressure/electricity. SMA = shape change with temperature.
💡 Examiner tip: Thermo = temperature, Photo = light, Piezo = pressure/electricity. Learn the prefix to remember the property.
QUESTION 3 1 mark
Which of the following best describes the circular economy?
A A system where products are made, used, and then sent to landfill
B A system designed to eliminate waste by keeping materials in use for as long as possible through reuse, repair, and recycling
C A system where all products are made from circular cross-sections
D A system where factories operate 24 hours a day in a cycle
✓ Correct! The circular economy aims to keep materials in use for as long as possible through reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling — contrasting with the linear "take-make-dispose" model.
💡 Examiner tip: Remember the 6Rs: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Rethink, Repair, Refuse. The circular economy encompasses all of these.
QUESTION 4 1 mark
What is the main advantage of using industrial robots for car body spot welding?
A Robots are cheaper to buy than human workers
B Robots can work in any environment without any programming
C Robots provide consistent quality, high speed, and can work continuously without fatigue
D Robots produce stronger welds than human welders
✓ Correct! Robots provide consistent quality (every weld identical), high speed, and can operate 24/7 without fatigue. They are NOT necessarily cheaper to buy — they have high capital costs.
💡 Examiner tip: Robot advantages = consistency, speed, 24/7 operation, hazardous environments, precision. Disadvantages = high capital cost, programming required, inflexible for small batches.
QUESTION 5 1 mark
A company designs a phone with a glued-in battery that cannot be replaced. This is an example of:
A Design for disassembly
B Circular economy design
C Planned obsolescence
D Life cycle assessment
✓ Correct! Planned obsolescence is designing products so they cannot be repaired or upgraded, forcing consumers to buy new products. A non-replaceable battery means the whole phone must be replaced when the battery degrades.
💡 Examiner tip: Planned obsolescence is a negative practice from a sustainability perspective. Design for disassembly is the opposite — making products easy to repair and recycle.

Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of using CNC machining compared to manual machining for producing 500 identical aluminium brackets. Consider: quality, cost, time, and skill requirements.
Model Answer — Level Marked (6 marks)
CNC Advantages:
• Consistent quality — every bracket identical, tight tolerances maintained throughout all 500 parts (1)
• Faster production — CNC can run continuously without breaks (1)
• No skilled machinist needed during production — just setup (1)
• Complex features can be machined automatically (1)

CNC Disadvantages:
• High initial cost of CNC machine and CAM software (1)
• Programming time required before production can start (1)
• Machine maintenance and tooling costs (1)

Manual Machining Advantages:
• Lower initial capital cost (1)
• Flexible — skilled machinist can adapt to variations (1)
• No programming required (1)

Manual Disadvantages:
• Inconsistent quality — human error, fatigue (1)
• Slower — cannot run unattended (1)
• Requires highly skilled machinist (1)

Conclusion: For 500 identical parts, CNC is more appropriate despite higher setup costs, as the cost per part is lower, quality is consistent, and production is faster. (1)
💡 Examiner tip: For evaluate questions, always discuss BOTH sides and reach a justified conclusion. The conclusion should reference the specific context (500 identical parts = high enough volume to justify CNC setup cost).
