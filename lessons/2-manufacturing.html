Additive Manufacturing

3.2.1 — Building objects layer by layer

Additive manufacturing (AM) builds objects by adding material layer by layer, guided by a digital 3D model (CAD file). Unlike subtractive processes, it produces very little waste material.

🖨️ FDM — Fused Deposition Modelling

Material: Thermoplastic filament (ABS, PLA, Nylon)

Process: Filament is fed through a heated nozzle, melted, and deposited in layers on a build platform. Each layer fuses to the previous one as it cools.

Uses: Prototyping, consumer products, medical models, educational models.

Advantages: Cheap, easy to use, wide material choice.

Disadvantages: Visible layer lines, lower strength than injection moulding, slow for large parts.

⚡ SLS — Selective Laser Sintering

Material: Metal or polymer powder (steel, titanium, nylon)

Process: A laser sinters (fuses) powder particles together layer by layer. Unsintered powder supports the part during building (no support structures needed).

Uses: Metal parts for aerospace, medical implants, complex geometries impossible to machine.

Advantages: Can produce complex internal features, strong metal parts.

Disadvantages: Very expensive machines, requires post-processing.

💧 SLA — Stereolithography

Material: Photopolymer resin

Process: UV laser cures (hardens) liquid resin layer by layer. Produces very high surface finish and accuracy.

Uses: Dental models, jewellery prototypes, highly detailed models.

Advantages: Excellent surface finish, high accuracy.

Disadvantages: Resins can be brittle, expensive materials, requires UV post-curing.

📌 Key Exam Points — Additive Manufacturing

Advantages of AM over traditional manufacturing: Can produce complex geometries impossible to machine; near-zero material waste; no tooling costs; rapid prototyping (from CAD to part in hours); easy customisation (each part can be different).

Disadvantages: Slower than injection moulding for high volumes; surface finish often requires post-processing; material properties may be anisotropic (weaker in Z-direction for FDM); high machine costs for metal AM.

📐 Cutting Speed Formula

The AQA specification requires you to calculate cutting speeds and spindle speeds.

Cutting Speed
v = π × D × N ÷ 1000
v = cutting speed (m/min) · D = diameter (mm) · N = spindle speed (rpm)

Rearranged for spindle speed: N = (v × 1000) ÷ (π × D)

Example: A 20mm diameter milling cutter cuts aluminium at 200 m/min. N = (200 × 1000) ÷ (π × 20) = 3183 rpm.

Casting & Moulding

3.2.4 — Sand casting, die casting, injection moulding

Casting and moulding processes involve pouring or injecting a liquid material into a mould, where it solidifies to form the desired shape. These are ideal for producing complex shapes in large quantities.

Joining & Assembly

3.2.5 — Permanent and temporary fastening methods
MethodTypeProcessMaterialsUses
Riveting Permanent A rivet (soft metal pin) is inserted through holes and the tail is deformed (bucked) to clamp the joint. Aluminium, steel Aircraft fuselage, bridges, structural steelwork
Threaded Fastenings Temporary Bolts, screws, and nuts. Can be assembled and disassembled. Metals, polymers Machine assembly, car engines, furniture
Soft Soldering Permanent Filler metal (tin-lead or tin-silver alloy) melted at <450°C flows into joint by capillary action. Flux prevents oxidation. Copper, brass, PCBs Electronic circuit boards, plumbing joints
Hard Soldering (Brazing) Permanent Brass filler rod melted at 450–900°C. Stronger than soft soldering. Uses flux. Steel, copper, brass Bicycle frames, copper pipe fittings, jewellery
MIG/TIG Welding Permanent Electric arc melts base metal and filler wire. Shielding gas (argon/CO₂) prevents oxidation. Very strong joint — can be stronger than parent metal. Steel, aluminium, stainless steel Car bodies, structural steelwork, pipelines
Spot Welding Permanent Electrical resistance heating at electrode contact points fuses thin metal sheets together. Fast and automated. Thin steel sheet Car body panels, white goods (washing machines)
⚠️ Soldering vs Brazing vs Welding

Soldering (<450°C) — weakest, used for electronics. Brazing (450–900°C) — stronger, used for structural joints without melting base metal. Welding (>1000°C) — strongest, melts base metal, used for structural applications. The key difference: welding melts the parent metal; soldering and brazing do not.

QUESTION 1 1 mark
Which manufacturing process is most suitable for producing one million identical plastic bottle caps?
A Sand casting
B FDM 3D printing
C Injection moulding
D Pressure die casting
✓ Correct! Injection moulding is ideal for high-volume thermoplastic parts. Once the mould is made, each part costs very little to produce.
💡 Examiner tip: Die casting is for metals, not plastics. Sand casting is for one-off/small batch. 3D printing is too slow for millions of parts.
QUESTION 2 1 mark
A student wants to join two pieces of copper pipe. Which joining method is most appropriate?
A MIG welding
B Soft soldering
C Riveting
D Spot welding
✓ Correct! Soft soldering is the standard method for joining copper water pipes. It uses a tin-based solder at low temperature.
💡 Examiner tip: Welding copper is difficult and unnecessary for pipes. Riveting is for sheet metal. Spot welding is for thin steel sheets.
QUESTION 3 1 mark
What is the main advantage of additive manufacturing (3D printing) over traditional subtractive manufacturing?
A It produces parts faster than injection moulding
B It is cheaper for mass production
C It produces near-zero material waste and can create complex internal geometries
D It produces stronger parts than casting
✓ Correct! AM produces near-zero waste (only builds what is needed) and can create internal features (hollow structures, lattices) impossible to machine.
💡 Examiner tip: AM is NOT faster than injection moulding for high volumes. Its advantages are: no tooling cost, complex geometries, customisation, and low waste.
QUESTION 4 1 mark
Which surface finishing process involves coating steel with a layer of zinc to prevent corrosion?
A Electroplating
B Galvanising
C Dip coating
D Anodising
✓ Correct! Galvanising involves dipping steel into molten zinc. The zinc coating provides sacrificial protection — it corrodes before the steel does.
💡 Examiner tip: Galvanising = zinc on steel. Electroplating = any metal deposited by electrolysis. Dip coating = plastic powder on metal.
QUESTION 5 1 mark
In sand casting, what is the purpose of the riser?
A To hold the two halves of the mould together
B To provide the channel through which molten metal is poured
C To allow gases to escape and provide a reservoir of molten metal to compensate for shrinkage
D To create a hollow section in the casting
✓ Correct! The riser serves two purposes: it allows trapped gases to escape, and it provides a reservoir of molten metal to feed the casting as it shrinks during solidification.
💡 Examiner tip: The SPRUE is the pour hole. The RUNNER connects the sprue to the cavity. The RISER is the vent/feed reservoir. The CORE creates hollow sections.

★★ Medium Style: AQA 8852 Short Answer 4 marks
Describe the process of pressure die casting. Include in your answer: the mould used, the material, and two advantages over sand casting.
Model Answer (4 marks)
Pressure die casting uses a permanent steel die/mould (1) into which molten non-ferrous metal (e.g., aluminium or zinc) is injected under high pressure (1).

Two advantages over sand casting (1 mark each):
• Much better surface finish — parts may not need machining (1)
• Higher dimensional accuracy / tighter tolerances (1)
• Faster production rate / suitable for mass production (1)
• Permanent mould can be used thousands of times (1)
💡 Examiner tip: Always state the material type (non-ferrous) and the mould type (permanent steel die) for full marks. For advantages, compare specifically to sand casting.
