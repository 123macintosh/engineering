Section A — Multiple Choice & Short Answer

Questions 1–10 · 20 marks total · Approximately 30 minutes
Q1 1 mark
Which of the following is a thermosetting polymer?

A — Polypropylene (PP)    B — Polyethylene (HDPE)    C — Epoxy resin    D — Acrylic (PMMA)
Model Answer — 1 mark
C — Epoxy resin
Epoxy resin is a thermosetting polymer — it undergoes an irreversible chemical change when cured and cannot be re-melted. Polypropylene, HDPE, and acrylic are all thermoplastics.
💡 Thermosetting = irreversible cure (epoxy, polyester resin, phenol formaldehyde/Bakelite, melamine formaldehyde, urea formaldehyde). Thermoplastic = can be re-melted.
Q2 1 mark
A component is described as malleable. What does this mean?

A — It can be stretched into a wire    B — It can be hammered into shape without cracking    C — It returns to its original shape after deformation    D — It is resistant to scratching
Model Answer — 1 mark
B — It can be hammered into shape without cracking
Malleable = can be permanently deformed by compressive forces (hammering, rolling) without fracturing. Ductile = can be drawn into wire. Elastic = returns to original shape. Hard = resistant to indentation/scratching.
💡 Malleable ≠ Ductile. Malleable = compressive deformation (hammering). Ductile = tensile deformation (wire drawing). Gold is both highly malleable and ductile.
Q3 2 marks
State two reasons why aluminium alloys are used for aircraft fuselages rather than mild steel.
Model Answer — 2 marks (1 each)
Any two of:
• Lower density — aluminium is approximately 3× less dense than steel, reducing aircraft weight and improving fuel efficiency (1)
• Good strength-to-weight ratio — aluminium alloys are strong relative to their weight (1)
• Corrosion resistance — aluminium forms a natural oxide layer that protects against corrosion (1)
• Good machinability and formability — can be formed into complex fuselage shapes (1)
💡 For "state two reasons" questions, give two distinct points. Don't repeat the same idea in different words. Each point must be clearly different to earn separate marks.
Q4 2 marks
Explain the difference between a first class lever and a second class lever. Give one example of each.
Model Answer — 2 marks
First class lever: The fulcrum is between the effort and the load (1). Example: scissors, crowbar, seesaw, pliers.

Second class lever: The load is between the fulcrum and the effort (1). Example: wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener.
💡 Remember: 1st class = fulcrum in middle (F-L-E or E-F-L). 2nd class = load in middle (F-L-E). 3rd class = effort in middle (F-E-L) — e.g., tweezers, fishing rod.
Q5 2 marks
A gear train has a driver gear with 15 teeth rotating at 600 rpm. The driven gear has 45 teeth.

(a) Calculate the gear ratio. [1 mark]
(b) Calculate the output speed of the driven gear. [1 mark]
Model Answer — 2 marks
(a) GR = T_driven ÷ T_driver = 45 ÷ 15 = 3 : 1 (1)

(b) Output speed = Input speed ÷ GR = 600 ÷ 3 = 200 rpm (1)
💡 GR > 1 = speed reduction, torque increase. GR < 1 = speed increase, torque reduction. Always write the formula first.
Q6 2 marks
Name the process shown in the diagram below and describe what happens to the material during this process.

[Diagram shows a metal billet being forced through a shaped die opening]
Model Answer — 2 marks
Extrusion (1)

A heated metal billet (or polymer) is forced under high pressure through a shaped die opening. The material takes the cross-sectional shape of the die and emerges as a continuous length with a constant cross-section (1).
💡 Extrusion = forcing material through a die (like squeezing toothpaste). Used for aluminium window frames, copper pipes, plastic profiles. The die determines the cross-section shape.
Q7 2 marks
A circuit contains a 9V battery and three resistors in series: 10Ω, 20Ω, and 15Ω.

(a) Calculate the total resistance. [1 mark]
(b) Calculate the current flowing through the circuit. [1 mark]
Model Answer — 2 marks
(a) R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ = 10 + 20 + 15 = 45 Ω (1)

(b) I = V ÷ R = 9 ÷ 45 = 0.2 A (1)
💡 Series: R_total = sum of all resistors. Same current through all. Voltage splits across resistors. V = I × R for each resistor.
Q8 2 marks
Describe the difference between Quality Control (QC) and Quality Assurance (QA).
Model Answer — 2 marks
Quality Control (QC) involves inspecting and testing finished products to identify defects — it is reactive, finding problems after they have occurred (1).

Quality Assurance (QA) involves systematic processes and procedures designed to prevent defects from occurring in the first place — it is proactive (1).
💡 Key distinction: QC = reactive (finds defects after). QA = proactive (prevents defects). ISO 9001 is a QA standard. Go/No-Go gauges are a QC tool.
Q9 2 marks
State two advantages of using CAD software compared to manual drawing on paper.
Model Answer — 2 marks (1 each)
Any two of:
• Designs can be easily edited without redrawing (1)
• Designs can be shared electronically worldwide (1)
• Automatic checking for errors and clashes in assemblies (1)
• Parametric modelling — changing one dimension updates the whole model (1)
• Can be directly linked to CAM for CNC manufacturing (1)
• 3D visualisation and photorealistic rendering (1)
💡 Don't say "it's faster" without explaining why. Say "designs can be edited without redrawing" — that's the specific advantage that earns the mark.

Diagram Labelling — Pneumatic Circuit

The diagram below shows a simple pneumatic circuit. Label the four components indicated by arrows A, B, C, and D.
PNEUMATIC CIRCUIT DIAGRAM — Label each component

Section B — Materials & Manufacturing

Questions 11–15 · 20 marks total · Approximately 30 minutes
Q11 4 marks
A manufacturer needs to produce 10,000 identical plastic bottle caps. Suggest a suitable manufacturing process and explain why it is appropriate for this production volume. Include in your answer: the process name, how it works, and two reasons why it is suitable.
Model Answer — 4 marks
Injection moulding (1)

Molten thermoplastic is injected under high pressure into a closed mould. The plastic cools and solidifies, and the mould opens to eject the finished part (1).

Suitable because:
• High production rate — cycle times of 10–30 seconds, so 10,000 parts can be produced quickly (1)
• Consistent quality — every part is identical to the mould shape, tight tolerances maintained (1)
• Low cost per part at high volumes — high tooling cost is spread over many parts (1)
• Complex shapes with undercuts and internal features can be produced (1)
💡 For "suggest and justify" questions: name the process (1 mark), describe how it works (1 mark), then give specific reasons linked to the context (volume, material, shape). Don't just say "it's fast" — say "cycle time of ~20 seconds allows 10,000 parts to be produced in under 2 days."
Q12 4 marks
The table below shows properties of four materials. A bicycle frame designer needs a material that is lightweight, strong, and corrosion resistant.

MaterialDensity (g/cm³)Tensile Strength (MPa)Corrosion ResistanceCost
Mild Steel7.85400PoorLow
Aluminium 60612.70310ExcellentMedium
Carbon Fibre (CFRP)1.601500ExcellentVery High
Titanium Ti-6Al-4V4.43950ExcellentHigh
(a) Calculate the specific strength (strength ÷ density) of aluminium 6061. [1 mark]
(b) Recommend the most suitable material for a high-performance racing bicycle frame where cost is not a constraint. Justify your answer using data from the table. [3 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) Specific strength = 310 ÷ 2.70 = 114.8 MPa·cm³/g (or 114.8 MPa/(g/cm³)) (1)

(b) Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) (1)

Justification: CFRP has the highest specific strength of all four materials (1500 ÷ 1.60 = 937.5 MPa·cm³/g), meaning it provides the greatest strength for the least weight (1). It also has excellent corrosion resistance, which is important for outdoor use (1). Although it is the most expensive, the question states cost is not a constraint (1).
💡 For data-based questions, always reference specific numbers from the table. "CFRP has the highest specific strength of 937.5 compared to 114.8 for aluminium" is much better than "CFRP is stronger and lighter."
Q13 4 marks
Describe the process of sand casting for producing a metal component. Your answer should include: preparation of the mould, pouring of metal, and removal of the casting. [4 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
1. A pattern (replica of the component, slightly oversized to allow for shrinkage) is placed in a moulding box (flask) and packed with damp sand mixed with a binder (1).

2. The pattern is removed, leaving a cavity in the sand. A runner (channel for molten metal to flow in) and riser (overflow channel to indicate when mould is full and to compensate for shrinkage) are formed (1).

3. The two halves of the mould (cope and drag) are assembled. Molten metal is poured into the runner until it appears in the riser (1).

4. The metal is allowed to cool and solidify. The sand mould is broken apart (destroyed) to remove the casting. The runner and riser are cut off and the casting is cleaned (fettled) (1).
💡 Sand casting key terms: pattern, cope, drag, runner, riser, parting line, core (for internal cavities), fettling. The mould is destroyed each time — one-off process.
Q14 4 marks
A steel shaft is heat treated by case hardening.

(a) Explain what case hardening achieves and why it is used for a shaft. [2 marks]
(b) Describe the process of carburising as a method of case hardening. [2 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) Case hardening produces a hard outer surface (case) while maintaining a tough, ductile core (1). This is ideal for a shaft because the surface must resist wear (from bearings, gears) while the core must absorb impact loads without fracturing (1).

(b) Carburising: The low-carbon steel component is heated to approximately 900°C in a carbon-rich atmosphere (e.g., charcoal, gas) (1). Carbon diffuses into the surface layer, increasing the carbon content of the outer case. The component is then quenched (rapidly cooled) to harden the high-carbon case (1).
💡 Case hardening = hard outside, tough inside. Used for gears, shafts, camshafts. Methods: carburising (add carbon), nitriding (add nitrogen), flame/induction hardening (heat and quench surface only).
Q15 4 marks
Compare MIG welding and TIG welding. In your answer, describe how each process works and state one advantage of each. [4 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
MIG Welding (Metal Inert Gas): A continuously fed consumable wire electrode melts and forms the weld pool. An inert shielding gas (argon or CO₂) protects the weld from atmospheric contamination (1). Advantage: Fast, easy to use, suitable for thick materials and long welds — widely used in automotive manufacturing (1).

TIG Welding (Tungsten Inert Gas): A non-consumable tungsten electrode creates an arc. Filler metal is added separately by hand. Argon shielding gas is used (1). Advantage: Produces very high-quality, precise welds with excellent appearance — suitable for thin materials, stainless steel, and aluminium (aerospace, food industry) (1).
💡 MIG = fast, easy, thick materials, automotive. TIG = slow, skilled, thin materials, high quality, aerospace. Both use inert gas shielding. MIG = consumable electrode. TIG = non-consumable tungsten electrode.

Section C — Systems & Calculations

Questions 16–20 · 20 marks total · Approximately 30 minutes
Q16 4 marks
A pneumatic cylinder has a bore diameter of 100mm and a rod diameter of 40mm. The supply pressure is 6 bar.

(a) Calculate the force produced on the forward (extend) stroke. [2 marks]
(b) Calculate the force produced on the return (retract) stroke. [2 marks]
Note: 1 bar = 100,000 Pa
Model Answer — 4 marks
P = 6 × 100,000 = 600,000 Pa

(a) Forward stroke — full bore area:
r = 100 ÷ 2 = 50mm = 0.05m
A_bore = π × 0.05² = π × 0.0025 = 0.007854 m² (1)
F = P × A = 600,000 × 0.007854 = 4,712 N ≈ 4.7 kN (1)

(b) Return stroke — annular area (bore minus rod):
r_rod = 40 ÷ 2 = 20mm = 0.02m
A_rod = π × 0.02² = 0.001257 m²
A_annular = 0.007854 − 0.001257 = 0.006597 m² (1)
F = 600,000 × 0.006597 = 3,958 N ≈ 4.0 kN (1)
💡 Forward stroke = full bore area. Return stroke = bore area MINUS rod area (annular area). The rod takes up space, so less area for pressure to act on = less force on return.
Q17 4 marks
A transformer has 500 turns on the primary coil and 50 turns on the secondary coil. The primary voltage is 230V.

(a) Calculate the secondary (output) voltage. [2 marks]
(b) If the secondary current is 10A, calculate the primary current (assume 100% efficiency). [2 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p (1)
V_s = V_p × (N_s/N_p) = 230 × (50/500) = 230 × 0.1 = 23V (1)

(b) At 100% efficiency: P_in = P_out, so V_p × I_p = V_s × I_s (1)
I_p = (V_s × I_s) / V_p = (23 × 10) / 230 = 230 / 230 = 1 A (1)
💡 Transformer ratio: V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p = I_p/I_s. Step-down transformer (fewer secondary turns) = lower voltage, higher current. Step-up = higher voltage, lower current.
Q18 4 marks
A worm gear drive has a worm with 1 start (1 tooth equivalent) and a worm wheel with 40 teeth. The worm rotates at 1,500 rpm with an input torque of 2 Nm.

(a) Calculate the gear ratio. [1 mark]
(b) Calculate the output speed of the worm wheel. [1 mark]
(c) Calculate the output torque (assuming 80% efficiency). [2 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) GR = teeth on worm wheel ÷ starts on worm = 40 ÷ 1 = 40 : 1 (1)

(b) Output speed = 1500 ÷ 40 = 37.5 rpm (1)

(c) Ideal output torque = Input torque × GR = 2 × 40 = 80 Nm (1)
Actual output torque = 80 × (80/100) = 80 × 0.8 = 64 Nm (1)
💡 Worm gears: GR = worm wheel teeth ÷ worm starts. Very high gear ratios in compact space. Self-locking (cannot be back-driven). Used in lifts, conveyor drives. Always apply efficiency to get actual output.
Q19 4 marks
A logic gate circuit uses an AND gate and a NOT gate (inverter). Input A = 1, Input B = 0.

(a) Complete the truth table for a 2-input AND gate. [2 marks]
(b) If the output of the AND gate is fed into a NOT gate, what is the final output when A=1 and B=1? [1 mark]
(c) What is the name of the combined AND + NOT gate? [1 mark]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) AND gate truth table (1 mark for all 4 rows correct):
A=0, B=0 → Output=0
A=0, B=1 → Output=0
A=1, B=0 → Output=0
A=1, B=1 → Output=1 (1)
(AND only outputs 1 when BOTH inputs are 1) (1)

(b) When A=1, B=1: AND output = 1. NOT gate inverts: final output = 0 (1)

(c) AND + NOT = NAND gate (1)
💡 Logic gate mnemonics: AND = ALL inputs must be 1. OR = ANY input can be 1. NOT = inverts. NAND = AND then NOT. NOR = OR then NOT. XOR = inputs must be DIFFERENT.
Q20 4 marks
A block and tackle pulley system has 4 rope sections supporting the load.

(a) State the Velocity Ratio of this system. [1 mark]
(b) A load of 800N is lifted using an effort of 240N. Calculate the Mechanical Advantage. [1 mark]
(c) Calculate the efficiency of the system. [2 marks]
Model Answer — 4 marks
(a) VR = number of rope sections supporting the load = 4 (1)

(b) MA = Load ÷ Effort = 800 ÷ 240 = 3.33 (1)

(c) Efficiency = (MA ÷ VR) × 100 (1)
Efficiency = (3.33 ÷ 4) × 100 = 83.3% (1)
💡 For pulleys: VR = number of rope sections supporting the load. Efficiency < 100% due to friction in the pulley bearings and weight of the pulleys themselves.

Section D — Extended Response

Questions 21–23 · 20 marks total · Approximately 30 minutes
Q21 6 marks
Evaluate the use of composite materials compared to metals in the manufacture of sports equipment such as bicycle frames and tennis rackets. In your answer, consider: properties, manufacturing, cost, and sustainability. [6 marks]
Model Answer — Level Marked (6 marks)
Properties:
• CFRP has an exceptional strength-to-weight ratio — stronger than steel at a fraction of the weight, critical for sports equipment where weight reduction improves performance (1)
• Composites can be engineered to be stiff in one direction and flexible in another (anisotropic), allowing designers to optimise performance (1)
• Metals (aluminium alloys) are isotropic — same properties in all directions — simpler to design with but less optimisable (1)

Manufacturing:
• Composites require skilled hand lay-up or autoclave processing — slow and expensive for complex shapes (1)
• Metals can be formed by extrusion, hydroforming, and welding — faster and more established processes (1)

Cost:
• CFRP is significantly more expensive than aluminium alloys — a CFRP bike frame costs 5–10× more than aluminium (1)

Sustainability:
• Aluminium is highly recyclable (95% energy saving vs. primary production) — better end-of-life sustainability (1)
• CFRP is difficult to recycle — fibres can be recovered but properties are degraded; most CFRP waste goes to landfill (1)

Conclusion: Composites offer superior performance properties but at higher cost and with poorer sustainability. For elite sports equipment, composites are preferred; for mass-market products, aluminium alloys offer a better balance of performance, cost, and sustainability. (1)
💡 Level 3 (5–6 marks): Detailed, balanced evaluation with specific data and a justified conclusion. Level 2 (3–4 marks): Some comparison with limited justification. Level 1 (1–2 marks): Basic statements about one material only.
Q22 6 marks
A car manufacturer is designing a new production line for assembling car bodies. Evaluate the use of industrial robots compared to human workers for the following tasks: spot welding, paint spraying, and final assembly (fitting interior components). Consider: quality, safety, flexibility, and cost. [6 marks]
Model Answer — Level Marked (6 marks)
Spot Welding — Robots preferred:
• Robots produce consistent, high-quality welds at every joint — no fatigue-related quality variation (1)
• Robots can work in confined spaces and at high temperatures without safety concerns (1)
• Cycle time is faster and robots can operate 24/7 (1)

Paint Spraying — Robots preferred:
• Robots apply paint with consistent thickness and coverage — no runs or misses (1)
• Paint spraying involves toxic fumes and flammable materials — robots eliminate human health and safety risks (1)

Final Assembly — Humans preferred:
• Fitting interior components requires dexterity, judgement, and adaptability — humans excel at handling varied components (1)
• Robots struggle with flexible materials (wiring looms, carpets) and non-repeatable tasks (1)
• Programming robots for every interior variant is complex and expensive (1)

Cost:
• High capital cost of robots (£100,000–£500,000 each) but lower ongoing labour costs (1)
• Human workers are more flexible and can be retrained for different tasks (1)

Conclusion: Robots are most appropriate for repetitive, hazardous tasks (welding, painting) where consistency is critical. Humans remain better for complex, variable assembly tasks requiring judgement and dexterity. Modern car plants use a combination of both. (1)
💡 Always address ALL the criteria mentioned in the question (quality, safety, flexibility, cost). Missing one criterion means you can't achieve the highest level. Structure your answer by task or by criterion — either approach works.
Q23 8 marks
Design question: A small engineering company needs to produce 50 aluminium brackets per week. Each bracket requires: 3 drilled holes, 2 milled slots, and a bent flange. The company has a limited budget.

(a) Suggest a suitable production method for each operation (drilling, milling, bending). [3 marks]
(b) Describe how the company could use CAD/CAM to improve efficiency. [3 marks]
(c) Suggest two quality control methods the company should use and explain why each is appropriate. [2 marks]
Model Answer — 8 marks
(a) Suitable methods (1 each):
• Drilling: Pillar drill or CNC drilling — pillar drill is low cost for small batches; CNC ensures consistent hole positions (1)
• Milling: CNC milling machine — ensures consistent slot dimensions across all 50 brackets (1)
• Bending: Press brake / folder — applies a controlled bending force to create the flange at a consistent angle (1)

(b) CAD/CAM benefits (1 each):
• CAD software (e.g., Fusion 360) is used to create a precise 3D model of the bracket with all dimensions and tolerances (1)
• CAM software generates toolpaths and G-code for the CNC milling machine automatically, reducing programming time (1)
• The same CAD model can be used to generate inspection drawings for quality control, ensuring all features are checked against the original design (1)

(c) QC methods (1 each):
• Go/No-Go gauge for the drilled holes — quickly checks if hole diameters are within tolerance without measuring each one; fast and suitable for batch production (1)
• Vernier caliper / micrometer for slot dimensions — provides precise measurements to verify slot width and depth against the drawing specification (1)
💡 For design questions, always link your choice to the specific context. 50 brackets/week = small batch production. Limited budget = avoid expensive high-volume tooling. CNC is appropriate because it offers consistency without the high tooling cost of injection moulding.
